Success

Personal reflections on events in my life that I consider to be successes.

Definition of success: (noun) the accomplishment of an aim or purpose

Below I have listed some successes in approximate chronological order:

Break the Cycle of Poverty and Self-Destruction

I vowed early in life to be the exact opposite of my parents. I never viewed them as role models. In fact, I viewed them as degenerates. Everything about them was a cautionary tale of selfishness, profligacy, addiction, and wreckless impulse. Growing up I received very little guidance and a lot of neglect. I didn't know which way was morally right, but I knew their ways were definitely wrong. My justifications were my direct and indirect sufferings. The greatest evil is causing the unnecessary suffering of others. I refused to be that evil to my children, family, friends, neighbors, and fellow members of society. Via Negativa (latin for 'the negative way') is a beautifully simplistic idea that I owe my life to.

Join the Air Force

I graduated high school one semester early by attending regular classes from 8:30am-3:30pm and then completing self-paced, online courses from 4pm-9pm. Early graduation gave me the opportunity to join the United States Air Force at the age of 17. While my friends were walking the stage, I was learning how to fix multi-million dollar aircraft. During this time I received three advanced promotions. I made E-4 below-the-zone (six months early), and I was the number 3 selectee across the entire Air Force for E-5...by the age of 19.

Become a Rescue Scuba Diver

Scuba diving is a popular activity in Okinawa, Japan. I was stationed there for three years while on active duty. During this time, I got my PADI open water scuba diver certification. I found a dive buddy who was equally passionate and we explored every dive site we could find around the island. Dive safety is a big concern because such an activity is inherently dangerous. I've been to 128 feet of depth with nothing but an air regulator to keep me breathing. That's scary. And being so adventurous, my buddy and I often went night diving. After realizing the risks first-hand, and hearing stories of other active duty members dying, we decided to become NAUI certified rescue scuba divers. Going through rigorous rescue dive training gave us more awareness of risks and better decision making skills about when to enter the water and what to do while submerged. To this day, I've got 92 dives and have only had one safety incident, which I thankfully survived.

Run my own business

After separating from the Air Force, I decided to try my hand at entrepreneurship by starting a shoe and clothing company. I found suppliers for sneakers, boots, polo shirts, jeans, shorts, t-shirts, and printing materials. I then created my own t-shirt designs with photoshop, printed them, and heat-pressed them onto blank t-shirts. My custom-made t-shirts were distributed to independent clothiers across Charlotte. The radius of my shoe business extended beyond the state borders. People literally went crazy for my sneakers. I sold them in front of night clubs, simon malls, and sporting events. In the end, gross revenue from the trunk of my car was on the order of $250,000 USD in shoes and $60,000 USD in clothing. Eventually I liquidated in favor of going to college...but at a nice profit.

Attend College

I was the first in my family to graduate from a 4-year university program. At the time of enrollment, I didn't know what major I wanted. I only knew that I wanted the best return on my investment. I asked the admissions counselor what the hardest program was at UNC Charlotte. She said it was electrical engineering. I enrolled thinking that if I can do the hardest program they offer, then I can everything else by default. Surely the hardest degree would pay a decent wage until I figured out what I really wanted to do in life. So far the plan has worked.

Get an MBA

I applied to Stanford and Duke. Both denied me. The University of Miami offered me a 25% scholarship to attend their MBA program. After crunching the numbers, I felt the remaining expense of the educational experience was still too high. I negotiated the scholarship up to 50% and they accepted. The University of Miami offered me $40,000 USD to attend their MBA program. I crunched the numbers again, and thought to myself why not just shoot for 100%. I genuinely felt that I deserved it. I reached back out to the University of Miami and tried to negotiate a 100% scholarship. They declined. I didn't end up attending their program, but I still consider this a success because I learned some valuable lessons:

  • The Spanish word for business is negocios. The root of which is the same as negotiation. I was embodying business by virtue of this negotiation, and it worked...at least partially.
  • Make data-driven decisions, but let your intuition be the guide.
  • Never be afraid to walk away from a deal.

I am reapplying to the Fuqua School of Business at Duke University using this website as the basis for my application. I believe that garycooper.io demonstrates my hyper-dimensionality, skills, background, and ambition more than any hand-written essay possibly could do.

Become a Computer Scientist

From 2010 to 2013 I worked in the nuclear engineering field. I automated robots that processed weapons-grade plutonium, and I designed safety automation systems for nuclear reactors - all very politically sexy. Theoretically the work was a perfect fit due to my affinity for nuclear physics, but reality proved different. The extreme regulatory nature of the industry constantly stunted my ambition and innovative drive. I wanted to get out of the industry, but my skills didn't translate to other career opportunities that I found interesting, namely software. After the Fukushima incident the entire nuclear industry came under scrutiny. Germany pulling out caused a major negative shift in public opinion. My company lost a lot of work. All of my projects dried up and many engineers were laid off or put on furlough. I was one of the few engineers who remained intact.

However, I felt a sense of anxiety about my career. I felt trapped in the sense that I was too dependent on one company/industry/skillset: layoff was imminent, other companies weren't hiring, and my skills wouldn't translate well to other industries. I made a personal commitment to use my downtime at work to teach myself hard technical skills that would keep me in demand in any given city in the world; skills that would give me power at my fingertips. I went through five levels of management to get a free IDE installed on my desktop at work. I bought an Oracle book on Java-based programming and taught myself. I started at page one and went through every lesson in the book.

After nine weeks of not having nuclear project work, I finally got laid off. Refusing to go back into the nuclear industry, I submitted [263] applications for software engineering jobs. All of my applications were auto-rejected. "While your qualifications are impressive, we've decided that you're not a fit at this time...". Eventually I learned how to tailor my resume for the job that I wanted and landed my first software gig. Then I branched out to learn C# and Javascript. Within my first year as a software engineer, I learned over 18 technologies. Since then I've spent thousands of hours studying and practicing Computer Science and have more than doubled my income. At this point in my career, I've mounted just over 10,000 hours and consider myself to have entered the "Mastery" or "Craftsman" phase.

Learn Martial Arts

I spent three months training at a professional Muay Thai fight camp in Phuket, Thailand. I focused solely on sobriety, flexibility, strength, and hand-to-hand combat skills during this time. My training regimen consisted of yoga, muay thai, muay boran, and cross-fit for five hours per day, six days per week. After three months, I came home. My energy and confidence were high, and my life goals were narrowly focused and achievable. I felt like a new man. Since then, martial arts have become an integral part of my life. Seeking more esoteric wisdom, I've begun training Russian Systema. Systema has entered my spirit and changed my life in more ways than I could have ever imagined. I've learned the goal is not to fight, but to gain self-knowledge and use it to live a fulfilling life. We learn through the pain from the strikes we give and take. We learn from healing others through strikes. We learn that we can't live a fulfilling life if we are dead. The fight keeps us alive.

Become an Artificial Intelligence Engineer

Prior to applying for an MS in Computer Science specializing in Artificial Intelligence, I thought it would be a good idea to explore my aptitude and interest in the subject. I signed up for a four-course Machine Learning specialization via Coursera. This coursework is taught by two Amazon professors of Machine Learning at the University of Washington. It covers foundational use cases, linear and logistic regression, and clustering and retrieval. I fell in love with ML. Once I completed those courses, I signed up for Andrew NG's Deep Learning specialization. This specialization includes five courses on the foundations of deep learning. The lectures are very rigorous, the math is hard, and the programming exercises are even more difficult. Over the last year and a half, I have spent well over 1000 hours completing nine certifications in Machine Learning.

Learn to Dance

I travelled to Cuba, Costa Rica, and Colombia during the summer of 2017. This latin american experience had a profound impact on me. I truly started understanding the culture and became enamored. I fell in love with every facet of the culture, namely, people, food, music, language, and dancing. One particular event sparked my desire to learn latin dancing.

A group of us were having dinner in a restaurant located on a mountain top outside of Domical, CR. The view was gorgeous as the dining room overlooked the city and beach beneath. After dinner, my friend Pat (a caucasian from Seattle who had fully acculturated to el estilo de vida latino) made a request to the owner to play some salsa music. The dancefloor adjacent to the dining room filled up immediately. Random gentlemen paired themselves with random ladies and everyone began smiling and dancing salsa. Pure happiness permeated the air. I eagerly watched Pat as he confidently and ever so gently guided the young lady through a beautiful orchestration of ballroom salsa. She was so happy to be dancing, smiling the entire time, and giving exclamations of joy with every spin.

I felt completely segregated from the fun. I desperately wanted to feel what everyone else was feeling, but I was paralyzed by inadequacy. I didn't know to dance or lead a woman through a dance. I appreciated the experience of watching, but I decided on the spot that I would never in my life be left out of the fun again. When I came home I started taking dance lessons immediately in Salsa and Bachata. I've been going four hours per week consistently for a year and a half now. Finally, I am confident enough to walk up to any woman in the room, extend my hand, ask her to dance, and take responsibility for her entertainment for the next 3-5 minutes. That's a big deal because boredom is the death of any relationship with a woman.
